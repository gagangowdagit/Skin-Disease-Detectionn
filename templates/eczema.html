Eczema - Treatment and Recommendations

Note: First preference is to consult a skin doctor.

1. Prescription Treatments for Eczema

  • Topical Corticosteroids: These are the most common medications for treating eczema flare-ups. Examples include hydrocortisone and betamethasone, which help reduce inflammation and itchiness.
  • Topical Calcineurin Inhibitors (e.g., Tacrolimus, Pimecrolimus): Used as an alternative to steroids, these help reduce inflammation without the side effects of long-term steroid use.
  • Topical PDE4 Inhibitors (e.g., Crisaborole): These are non-steroidal creams that reduce inflammation and are suitable for mild eczema.
  • Moisturizers and Emollients: Regular use of thick, fragrance-free moisturizers helps maintain skin hydration and prevents flare-ups.
  • Oral Antihistamines: These can help control itching, especially at night. Antihistamines like diphenhydramine (Benadryl) can reduce the severity of itching and discomfort.
  • Oral Corticosteroids: In severe cases, a short course of oral steroids (e.g., prednisone) may be prescribed to reduce widespread inflammation.
  • Phototherapy (UVB Light Therapy): For moderate to severe eczema, light therapy may be used to calm inflammation and improve skin health.

These treatments focus on managing flare-ups and soothing the skin.

2. Diet Recommendations

* Foods to Include:

  • Omega-3 Fatty Acids: Found in fatty fish (salmon, mackerel), flaxseeds, and walnuts, omega-3s help reduce skin inflammation and improve skin barrier function.
  • Probiotic-Rich Foods: Yogurt, kefir, and other fermented foods may help balance the gut microbiome and improve skin conditions like eczema.
  • Vitamin D-Rich Foods: Vitamin D is essential for immune function and skin health. Foods like fortified milk, eggs, and sunlight exposure can support skin healing.
  • Antioxidant-Rich Foods: Fruits and vegetables high in antioxidants (e.g., berries, spinach, carrots) help combat oxidative stress and reduce inflammation in the skin.
  • Whole Grains: Foods like oats, quinoa, and brown rice help reduce inflammation and provide steady energy, supporting overall health.

* Foods to Avoid:

  • Dairy Products: Some people with eczema may find that dairy triggers flare-ups. Avoiding milk, cheese, and yogurt may help.
  • Processed Foods: High in additives and preservatives, processed foods can contribute to skin inflammation and irritation.
  • Eggs and Nuts: These are common food allergens that may worsen eczema for some individuals. If allergic, avoid these foods to reduce flare-ups.
  • Spicy and Acidic Foods: These may irritate the skin in some individuals. It's best to avoid foods like citrus fruits, tomatoes, and spicy dishes during flare-ups.

3. Clean Procedures and Hygiene

Common Care and Hygiene Tips:

  • Moisturize Regularly: Keeping the skin well-moisturized with fragrance-free creams and ointments is crucial to prevent dryness and flare-ups.
  • Gentle Cleansing: Use a mild, fragrance-free soap and lukewarm water when bathing or showering. Avoid hot water, as it can dry out the skin and trigger eczema flare-ups.
  • Avoid Scratching: Scratching can worsen eczema by causing the skin to break and become infected. Try using a cold compress or topical treatments to relieve itching.
  • Wear Soft, Breathable Fabrics: Clothing made from cotton is gentler on the skin and reduces irritation. Avoid rough fabrics like wool that can cause further discomfort.
  • Use Mild Laundry Detergents: Fragrance-free and hypoallergenic laundry detergents reduce the risk of skin irritation.

4. Lifestyle Modifications

  • Reduce Stress: Emotional stress can trigger eczema flare-ups. Practices like yoga, meditation, or mindfulness can help reduce stress levels and improve skin health.
  • Keep the Skin Cool: Heat and sweating can trigger eczema flare-ups. Wear breathable clothing and stay cool, especially in hot weather.
  • Stay Hydrated: Drink plenty of water to keep the skin hydrated and maintain overall health.
  • Avoid Known Triggers: If certain substances (such as certain fabrics, soaps, or allergens) trigger your eczema, make sure to avoid them whenever possible.
  • Regular Skin Checks: Monitor your skin regularly for any signs of infection, as eczema can make the skin more susceptible to bacterial infections.
